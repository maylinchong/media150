Reading 3

“Graphic Novel: Motherhood in Crisis”
by Laurence Ivil, Alicia Prager & Saidu Bah

Reflection

“Motherhood in Crisis” uses the graphic novel format to explore the emotional and physical struggles faced by women during pregnancy and childbirth in Sierra Leone. What stood out to me most is how the visual storytelling makes the crisis feel personal and immediate. Instead of presenting statistics or reports, the authors show individual women’s experiences—fear, exhaustion, grief, and resilience—through expressive drawings and dialogue. This makes the issue feel less like a distant problem and more like something happening to real people whose stories deserve attention.

One of the strongest ideas in the piece is how the crisis is not caused by a single factor but by a combination of poverty, lack of medical resources, and social expectations placed on women. The graphic novel shows how women often have to travel long distances to reach a clinic, only to find that there are no supplies, no electricity, or no trained staff available. These moments highlight how preventable many of the deaths are. The visuals make these failures feel even more heartbreaking because you can see the fear and uncertainty on the characters’ faces.

Another important theme is how women carry the emotional weight of motherhood long before the child is even born. The story shows how they worry about their health, their family’s survival, and the possibility of dying during childbirth. The graphic format captures these emotions in a way that text alone might not. The authors use shadows, close-up panels, and symbolic imagery to show how heavy these fears can be. It made me think about how motherhood is often idealized, while the real dangers many women face are ignored.

The graphic novel also emphasizes community support. Even though the healthcare system is failing them, women rely on each other—sharing advice, comforting one another, and stepping in when help is needed. This sense of solidarity is powerful, but it also shows how unfair it is that women must depend on each other instead of receiving proper medical care.

Overall, “Motherhood in Crisis” made me reflect on how storytelling formats like graphic novels can make social issues more accessible and emotionally impactful. By combining images and narrative, the authors create a deeper understanding of the maternal health crisis. It reminds readers that behind every statistic is a real person with hopes, fears, and a story that deserves to be heard.
