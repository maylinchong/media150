Reading 2

“AI Slop Distorting Our Reality” by Nesrine Malik

Reflection

In “AI Slop Distorting Our Reality, the World Is Sleepwalking into Disaster,” Nesrine Malik argues that the large amount of AI-generated images online is not only filling our feeds but also changing how we understand reality. One idea that stood out to me is that we now live between two kinds of images: real pictures of the world and artificial ones made to entertain, shock, or influence people. The main problem, Malik explains, is not just that these images are fake, but that there are so many of them and they are so emotionally strong that they affect how we see what is real and important.

One of the most concerning parts of the essay is Malik’s discussion of political AI images. These images do not only confuse reality, but they also create new and false versions of it. They show made-up situations that support certain beliefs or ideas. These images can feel convincing because they look real and are easy to share, especially on platforms like WhatsApp, where there is little space for public discussion or correction. Malik gives the example of her elderly relative who believed AI-generated images about Sudan. This shows how trust, emotion, and technology can work together to make false information seem true. It also made me think about how images often feel more powerful than words. People tend to believe what they see, even when it is not real.

Malik also discusses what she calls the “structural conservatism” of AI, which is another important idea. Because AI systems are trained on existing data, they often repeat the same biases found in the past. This means they can create images that show narrow and outdated ideas, such as traditional gender roles, idealized families, or limited representations of society. These images may seem harmless, but they can support harmful ideas without directly arguing for them. Instead, they present these ideas in a soft and appealing way. People do not question them as much because they are presented as simple or attractive images rather than clear opinions.

At the same time, Malik explains that not all AI-generated content is political. Much of it is created simply to attract attention. Online platforms benefit from large amounts of cheap and easy content that keeps users engaged. Because of this, people are constantly exposed to many different types of images. Serious images of real events appear next to light or unrealistic content. Over time, this can make everything feel less important. People may become tired or emotionally distant, even when they see images of real suffering.

Overall, Malik’s essay made me think about how too many images can lead to confusion instead of understanding. People today are not lacking information, but they are overwhelmed by it. When it becomes difficult to tell what is real, it is easier to ignore everything. The idea of “sleepwalking into disaster” feels accurate because it describes how people slowly lose focus and concern. Malik’s message is not only about technology, but also about the need to question what we see and remain aware of how images shape our understanding of the world.
